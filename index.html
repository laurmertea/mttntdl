3. BSN

More about the BSN.

A BSN number is a personal identification number. The Dutch government uses this number to process your personal data. With a BSN number you can:

  • open a bank account,
  • apply for health insurance,
  • communicate with various government agencies. Your employer uses your BSN number to pay taxes and employer contributions.

! TIP: Are you coming to the Netherlands with family? Every family member will need their own BSN number. Make an appointment here.

! NEEDED:

  • valid passport or identity card (for a citizen of the European Union),
  • proof of residence in the Netherlands (rental contract, deed of sale or written authorization from the main resident).

! IMPORTANT: Current waiting times at the municipalities and RNI (registration for non-residents) locations is longer than usual (1-2 months), so keep this in mind.

4. DigiD

More about the DigiD.

With a DigiD you can identify yourself online to the government, the tax authority, healthcare institutions and pension funds.

! TIP: Requesting a DigiD is not obligatory, but strongly recommended.

! TIP: A DigiD gives access to confidential information about you, so you should store your login details in a safe place.

! NEEDED:

  • BSN,
  • Dutch home address.

! HOW TO:

  • navigate to DigiD application and go through the application process,
  • activate your DigiD: you will receive a letter with an activation code within three working days after registering and you can activate your DigiD using the app; you can log in after activating your DigiD.

! IMPORTANT: You must activate your DigiD within 20 days, otherwise you will have to go through the entire application process again.

5. Bank Account

More about the bank account.

You will need a Dutch bank account to pay your rent or mortgage, get a cell phone plan, pay utilities and receive your salary. In addition, Dutch shoppers and Dutch companies are using less and less cash.

! TIP: Use of credit cards in the Netherlands is not widespread. In many Dutch businesses and/or stores, for example in most Dutch supermarkets, it isn’t even possible to pay with a credit card.

! IMPORTANT: In the Netherlands you can get money from an automated teller using a debit card from another country, but you will be charged a fee.

! NEEDED:

  • valid passport or identity card (for a citizen of the European Union),
  • proof of address (e.g., a rental contract or utility bill).

6. Healthcare

More about the healthcare.

You are required to get Dutch health insurance. The same goes for family members that come to the Netherlands with you. The average costs for basic insurance are €100-€120 and are the same for everyone, regardless of age or health status.

! NEEDED:

  • BSN,
  • valid passport or identity card (for a citizen of the European Union),
  • Dutch bank account.

! HOW TO:

  • choose a health insurance plan that fits your needs and get it on the health insurer's website,
  • register with a general practitioner (PCP); you can find one near you here; from the moment your registration is confirmed and you actually want to see the doctor, you can make an appointment.

! IMPORTANT If the doctor considers it necessary, they will refer you to the hospital.

7. Insurances

More about the insurances.

In the Netherlands there are many insurance companies offering different types of insurance. Some of these insurances are legally required and others are optional, depending on your personal circumstances and preferences. These insurances listed below are optional.

! NEEDED:

  • Dutch bank account.

7.1. Home Contents Insurance

Home contents insurance covers damage to the contents of the home. Contents means all the things in the house that are not attached to it. The kitchen does not fall under home contents insurance, but kitchen appliances do. Home contents insurance costs between 8 and 15 euros per month, depending on living location and type of dwelling. Both renters and owners are advised to get home contents insurance. In some cases the landlord's insurance covers the contents.

8. Utilities

More about the utilities.

When you move to a new home (rental or owned), most of the time you will already have working electricity and/or gas, as well as running water. Still, you have to get a contract for these utilities. You can do this via Expat-Check.

The energy market in the Netherlands is privatized, which means that as a consumer you are free to choose from among multiple energy suppliers.

Water is supplied separately. Every region in the Netherlands has its own water authority. You cannot choose from several authorities. You have to register with the water management authority that is active in the region where you live.

You do not have to contract gas and electricity from the same supplier, although many energy suppliers offer electricity + gas package deals. There are also energy suppliers that specialize in either electricity or gas.

! NEEDED:

  • BSN,
  • valid Dutch address,
  • Dutch bank account.

! HOW TO:

  • check how your house/apartment is heated, for example with gas or a heat pump,
  • check whether solar panels have been placed on your house/apartment.
  • from the energy suppliers list, choose the one that fits your needs and sign a contract on the supplier’s website,
  • sign a contract with the water supplier that is active in your region.

! OPTIONS:

    • due to the many problems in the world, energy suppliers are reluctant to take on new customers,
    • at Budget Energie Budget Energie you can still conclude a contract,
    • energy costs are different for everyone. It depends on the size of your family and your usage.
    • advantages:
      • good prices,

    • disadvantages:

  • water:

9. Internet

More about the Internt plan.

You will have to arrange an internet and/or TV subscription yourself, unless you rent and this service is included in the rental price. Check this well in advance with the landlord.

The market for internet, TV and landline phones in the Netherlands is privatized, which means that as a consumer you are free to choose from multiple providers. Perhaps you want to get only internet or only TV.

In general, it is standard for serviced apartments in the Netherlands to provide internet + TV connectivity with a plan. The Netherlands has one of the top-5 fastest internet connections in the world.

! TIP: If these services are included, ask for a final statement once a year so that you know you aren't paying too much.

10. SIM

More about the SIM Only.

Even if your international mobile phone (with a plan) works just fine when you arrive, a Sim Only plan is strongly recommended. If you aren't using a Dutch telephone number, people (for example your employer or your neighbor) or businesses in the Netherlands who call you will end up paying for the costs. A Dutch phone number is also more reliable when you call Dutch individuals or businesses.

You will need to give a Dutch phone number for many services before you can use them, such as for PicNic, Ikea delivery, etc.

Without a Dutch phone number you cannot send a Tikkie (online payment request).
